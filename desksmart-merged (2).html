Ergonomic Workspace Guide

DeskSmart ergonomics guide

Build a healthier, more productive workspace.

A well-designed workstation can significantly reduce fatigue and long-term discomfort. You don't have to buy a lot of office tools — it's about making intentional adjustments that reduce strain, support posture, and keep your energy high through the day.

What is Ergonomics?

Ergonomics is the science of designing environments and tools to fit the people who use them — minimising physical stress, reducing injury risk, and optimising efficiency. The word itself comes from Greek roots:

ergon — work nomos — natural law

Put together: the natural laws of work. Good ergonomics means your workspace adapts to you, not the other way around.

01

Chair & Equipment

The right equipment forms the foundation of an ergonomic setup. Invest in a few key items and position them precisely.

  • Ergonomic chair with adjustable seat height, tilt, and lumbar support.
  • Monitor arm or stand for easy eye-level alignment.
  • Low-profile ergonomic keyboard and a comfortable, full-size mouse.
  • Optional wrist rest and footrest based on your body proportions.
  • A sit-stand desk or converter to alternate positions throughout the day.

Quick check

When seated, your thighs should be parallel to the floor and feet flat — adjust seat height first before anything else.

02

Laptop Stand Angles

Raising your laptop screen to eye level is one of the single most effective ergonomic changes you can make. Pair it with an external keyboard and mouse.

15° — Light Tilt A gentle incline that keeps the screen readable while reducing downward neck bend. Good starting point for shorter sessions.
20° — Moderate Raise Brings the screen closer to eye level. Recommended for most full-day setups; pair with an external keyboard to keep wrists neutral.
25° — Full Raise Near-vertical position ideal when using the laptop as a secondary display. Eliminates neck strain almost entirely.
  • Always use an external keyboard when the laptop is raised.
  • Top of screen should align with or be just below eye level.
03

Posture Tips

Good posture isn't about sitting rigidly upright — it's about staying in a neutral, supported position that lets your muscles relax.

  • Keep your spine neutral and shoulders relaxed, not rounded forward.
  • Align elbows near your torso at roughly 90° while typing.
  • Keep wrists straight and supported to reduce repetitive strain.
  • Take movement breaks every 30–60 minutes to reset your body.
  • Avoid crossing your legs — keep feet flat on the floor or a footrest.

20-20-20 rule

Every 20 minutes, look 20 feet away for 20 seconds to ease eye strain.

Micro-breaks

Stand and stretch for 1–2 minutes each hour to restore circulation.

Breathing reset

Five deep breaths before meetings relaxes neck and shoulder tension you didn't know you were holding.

04

Laptop Screen Height

A flat laptop on a desk forces your neck to tilt down — one of the most common causes of neck and upper-back pain for laptop users. Raising the screen is the single biggest improvement you can make.

  • Use a laptop stand or stack of books to raise the screen so the top edge is at or just below eye level.
  • Once raised, always pair with an external keyboard and mouse — you can't type comfortably on a raised laptop.
  • Aim for roughly an arm's length of distance from your eyes to the screen (50–70 cm).
  • Tilt the lid back slightly (100–110°) to reduce glare and match your natural gaze angle.
  • If working from a café or without a stand, prop the back of the laptop on a bag or notebook as a quick fix.

Quick test

Close your eyes, sit up straight, then open them. Your gaze should land naturally on the top third of your laptop screen — if you're looking down, raise it higher.

05

Laptop Keyboard & Trackpad

The built-in laptop keyboard and trackpad are convenient but can cause strain when used for long sessions. A few simple changes make a big difference.

  • When your laptop is raised on a stand, switch to an external keyboard placed at elbow height — wrists should stay flat and neutral.
  • If using the built-in keyboard, keep the laptop flat on the desk (not angled) and sit close enough that your arms aren't reaching forward.
  • The trackpad encourages small, repetitive finger movements — an external mouse reduces this strain significantly for long work sessions.
  • Rest your palm lightly on the surface when using the trackpad, not just your fingertips, to distribute tension.
  • Use keyboard shortcuts wherever possible to reduce how much you rely on the trackpad.

Wrist position

Float wrists above the keyboard while typing — only rest them during pauses. This applies to both built-in and external laptop keyboards.

Trackpad vs. mouse

For sessions over an hour, a compact wireless mouse is worth it — trackpad-only use is a common cause of thumb and wrist fatigue.

Laptop positioning

If not using a stand, centre the laptop directly in front of you — never off to one side, which forces you to twist your neck and shoulders.

06

Lighting for Laptop Use

Laptop screens are smaller and often glossier than desktop monitors, making glare and brightness management especially important. Where and how you position your laptop matters as much as the settings.

  • Sit with the window to your side — never behind you (causes glare on screen) or in front of you (creates a blinding backlight).
  • Laptop screens are highly reflective — a matte screen protector is one of the cheapest and most effective fixes for glare.
  • Adjust screen brightness to roughly match the brightness of your surroundings, not at maximum.
  • Enable Night Shift (Mac) or Night Light (Windows) after sunset to reduce blue light and ease eye fatigue.
  • When working in dim environments, add a small desk lamp pointed at your workspace — not at the screen — to reduce the contrast between screen and surroundings.

Outdoor & café tip

Working outside? Increase brightness to max and sit with your back to the sun so it doesn't wash out the screen. A laptop hood or privacy screen can help in bright conditions.

07

Break Routines

Movement is medicine. Even the best ergonomic setup can't substitute for regular breaks that reset your body and mind.

  • Follow the 30-30 rule: 30 minutes seated, 30-second movement break.
  • Set a subtle recurring timer — habit loops beat willpower.
  • Shoulder rolls, neck tilts, and wrist circles address the most common tension points.
  • Walk during phone calls whenever possible.
  • Use a standing desk or converter to alternate positions 2–3× daily.

Pomodoro method

25 minutes focused work, 5 minutes break. After four cycles, take a longer 15–30 minute rest.

Hydration cue

Keep a water bottle at your desk — drinking regularly creates a natural bio-break rhythm.

End-of-day stretch

5 minutes of forward folds and chest openers at day's end prevents cumulative stiffness.

08

Eye Strain

Digital eye strain (also called Computer Vision Syndrome) affects the majority of people who work at screens — and it's almost entirely preventable.

  • Apply the 20-20-20 rule: every 20 min, look 20 ft away for 20 sec.
  • Blink consciously — screen time reduces blink rate by up to 60%.
  • Keep screen brightness matched to ambient light, not at maximum.
  • Reduce blue light in the evening with a warm colour filter.
  • Schedule regular eye exams — many people work with uncorrected minor prescriptions.

Screen distance

The closer the screen, the harder your eyes work to focus. Aim for 50–70 cm minimum.

Anti-fatigue font size

Increase your default font size by 2pt — reduced squinting is worth the minor layout shift.
